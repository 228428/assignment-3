ཕུ་ན་ཁ་རྫོང

This project began as an exploration into transforming 2D images into spatial, living forms using TouchDesigner. I wanted to create something that felt both structured and organic—almost like a floating pixel sculpture. The final look was inspired by the textures of traditional Bhutanese architecture, but reimagined through a digital lens. To start,I used a Constant CHOP to set the resolution (I chose 500), then connected it to a Null to stabilize the value. Before diving into instancing, I built a rendering network: I created a Box SOP, added a Transform SOP (with a uniform scale of 0.015), and brought it into a Geo COMP. I planned to animate this geo later on. Then, I added a Camera COMP, Light COMP, and a Render TOP, where I set the resolution to 1080x1080, the pixel format to 16-bit float (RGBA), and anti-aliasing to 16x—just enough for my laptops GPU. To clean up the look, I added an RGB Key to isolate the background and turn it fully black. A Luma Blur was used for subtle soft-focus effects, almost like tilt-shift photography. The magic started when I made two Ramp TOPs—one for red (vertical), and another for green (horizontal). I added Math TOPs to remap the values from -5 to 5, giving the particles more spatial spread. Then I used a Reorder TOP to merge channels and a Null to finalize the positioning data. This was plugged into the instancing parameters of the geo so that the red and green ramps controlled the X and Z translation. After adjusting the camera angle and light (switched to a Cone Light to hide grid edges), the grid began to look like it extended infinitely. For the color data, I brought in a Ramp-based image and used Math and Fit TOPs to scale and convert it to 32-bit float. I assigned this to the instance color parameters (RGB + Alpha), giving each shape color variation. Finally, I added subtle rotation using an expression: me.time.seconds * 0.05 which added slow, hypnotic movement to the whole composition. While I kept the patch relatively minimal, I also experimented with reaction diffusion and emboss textures for future iterations. This version, though, was all about getting the basics right and letting each cube breathe with light and rhythm. The process was a combination of visual intuition, technical layering, and a fair bit of trial and error—but that is what I love most about TouchDesigner.
