Bhutan's Landmark

Bhutan is a small yet deeply spiritual country nestled in the Eastern Himalayas, bordered by India and China. Known for its breathtaking mountain landscapes, ancient monasteries, and commitment to Gross National Happiness, Bhutan is one of the few nations in the world where traditional culture and Buddhist values remain closely woven into everyday life. The country has preserved much of its heritage through careful governance and a strong sense of national identity, visible in its architecture, clothing, language, and sacred rituals. Dzongs fortress monasteries are found across the country, acting as both spiritual centers and administrative buildings. These structures, with their massive white walls and intricately painted wooden windows, embody Bhutan's unique blend of serenity and strength.

Tashichoe Dzong

This is Tashichho Dzong (བཀྲ་ཤིས་ཆོས་རྫོང་), the iconic fortress in Bhutan's capital, Thimphu, where the King's office, key ministries, and the summer residence of the Je Khenpo are located. Originally built in 1641 by Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal and later rebuilt in the 1960s by the Third King without using a single nail or blueprint, it stands as a symbol of tradition and power. It's also the Dzong of my own Dzongkhag, the district I grew up in. I still remember being woken at 3 AM by my parents to attend Namgay Tongchoe (གནམས་དགའ་མཐོང་མཆོད་), the highest form of prayer you can do for yourself, walking half asleep toward flickering butter lamps and the low hum of chanting monks. Every year, the Dzong transforms into a vibrant stage for Thimphu Tshechu, one of Bhutan's grandest spiritual festivals. Nestled by the Wang Chhu River with its towering white walls and intricate woodwork, Tashichho Dzong is not just a historic landmark it's a living memory of my childhood, faith, and roots.

Memorial Chorten

This is the National Memorial Chorten, one of the most sacred and frequented landmarks in Thimphu. Built in 1974 in memory of His Majesty Jigme Dorji Wangchuck, the Third King of Bhutan and the father of modern Bhutan, the chorten stands not only as a monument of national reverence but as a daily spiritual anchor for the local community. From dawn to dusk, elderly devotees and young passersby alike walk in a clockwise direction around the stupa, spinning prayer wheels and murmuring mantras like Om Mani Padme Hum. That same direction of movement inspired the rotation in my visual work, reflecting how devotion is expressed not only through prayer, but through the act of movement itself measured, intentional, and circular. The chorten's golden spire catches the morning light, and its whitewashed body is framed by a garden filled with incense, flowers, and quiet reflection. It"s not just a place of worship, but a living pulse of the city gentle, grounding, and eternal.

Phunakha Dzong

This is Punakha Dzong (ཕུ་ན་ཁ་རྫོང་), one of Bhutan's most important and visually striking fortresses, located at the confluence of the Pho Chhu and Mo Chhu rivers. Known for its warm, almost tropical climate, Punakha rarely ever sees snow but the photo my father took shows a rare moment when it did, the first snowfall in my lifetime, and possibly in decades. The contrast between the white snow and the Dzong's usual bright, dry surroundings was striking. Built in 1637 by Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal, Punakha Dzong was once the capital of Bhutan and still serves as the winter residence of the Central Monastic Body. It's also the site of major events like royal weddings and the annual Punakha Tshechu. The structure's scale and location make it one of the most architecturally impressive dzongs in the country and seeing it under snow added a quiet, unexpected layer to a place so familiar.

Dochula

This is Dochula Pass (རྡོ་ཆ་ལ་), a high-mountain pass that sits at over 3,100 meters above sea level, just a short drive from Thimphu. It's one of the most scenic spots in Bhutan, offering panoramic views of the eastern Himalayas on clear days. For me, Dochula is more than just a viewpoint it's a place I've passed countless times on my way to Punakha, Wangdue, and beyond. It feels like a threshold between valleys, a quiet pause before the next journey. But it also carries deeper meaning. The 108 Druk Wangyal Chortens that crown the pass were built in honor of the Bhutanese soldiers who lost their lives during Operation All Clear in 2003, when the Royal Bhutan Army successfully cleared Indian insurgent camps from southern Bhutan. It's a reminder that even in a peaceful country like ours, sacrifices have been made to preserve that peace. Today, Dochula stands as both a memorial and a must visit destination where beauty, history, and reflection converge.

Wangdue Dzong

This is Wangdue Phodrang Dzong (དབང་འདུས་ཕོ་བྲང་རྫོང་), located on a ridge overlooking the convergence of the Punatsangchhu and Dangchhu rivers. The photo taken by my father is special because it captures a rare snowfall in Wangdue, a place known for its dry, breezy climate where snow is almost unheard of. Originally built in 1638 by Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal, the dzong played a key role in controlling the routes to central and eastern Bhutan. Tragically, it was destroyed by a fire in 2012, which gutted much of the historic structure. Since then, the government and local communities have been working to reconstruct the dzong using traditional materials and techniques, preserving its original architectural style. The image of it covered in snow after a time of restoration adds another layer to its long and resilient story.
